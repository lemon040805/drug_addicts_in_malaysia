Worldwide: About 284 million people used drugs in 2020—roughly 1 in 18 adults—and this is about a 26% increase since 2010. The most commonly used drug is cannabis (around 209 million people, about 4% of adults), followed by amphetamines (about 34 million people, roughly 0.7% of the global population).

Health impact: Drug use is linked to around 5% of all substance-related deaths and about 9% of the overall health burden (DALYs) from substance use.

During COVID-19 lockdowns: in many places, drug use among teenagers fell, likely because schools and social activities were closed.

Malaysia snapshot: National data (NHMS 2019) indicate marijuana is the most commonly used illicit drug, followed by kratom. Among adolescents aged 13–18 in 2020, amphetamine-type stimulants (ATS) were most common, then marijuana and opiates (AADK).

How Is The Situation Trending?

During 2020–2021 detections dipped as movement controls disrupted outreach, testing and reporting.

From 2022 onward detections rose as services reopened and enforcement normalised. These are detections, not prevalence so levels can shift with true use and with how actively services and enforcement operate.

Source: Statistics Of Drug And Substance Abuse And Addiction – Agensi Antidadah Kebangsaan (AADK)
Where Is it happening most?

Persistent hotspots Northern and northeast Peninsular states—Kelantan, Terengganu, Perlis and Kedah—tend to show the heaviest shading across years, forming a contiguous high-detection corridor.

Lower baseline East Malaysia (Sabah, Sarawak) and the Klang Valley core (Selangor) are generally lighter on the map relative to northern/eastern states.

Post-pandemic rebound Many states darken again from 2022 as outreach resumes and enforcement normalises after COVID-19 disruptions.

Small-area volatility Federal territories—especially Putrajaya and Labuan— can shift shading quickly year to year because small populations amplify changes in detections.

What Is Being Used?

ATS dominates across all years, widening its lead again after 2022. This likely reflects both continued demand for stimulants and active detection in this category.

Opiates trend downward from earlier years, with a modest rebound most recently. The category remains clearly below ATS by the end of the period.

Cannabis is rising from a low baseline, especially post-pandemic, but still accounts for a smaller share of detections than ATS or opiates.

Psychotropic pills & Others remain minor in level yet show steady growth after 2022—this may be influenced by shifts in policing focus, testing, or reporting.

Source: Statistics Of Drug And Substance Abuse And Addicts By Drug Category – Agensi Antidadah Kebangsaan (AADK)
Who is most affected?

Working-age shift Young adults led for most years, but by the latest
year middle-aged adults edge ahead—suggesting an ageing cohort or changes in detection
focus.

Youth down from the peak Rates for ages 15–24 have trended lower
since pre-pandemic and remain well below the two working-age groups.

Older adults rising from a low base Rates have climbed steadily but
still sit below the peak working-age groups.

Children minimal Detection rates among under-15s remain very low.

Sex disparity persists Men account for the vast majority of detections (order-of-magnitude
higher than women). Both sexes dipped in 2020–2021 and reached new highs
as services and enforcement normalised from 2022.

What happens to detected cases?

Rising share of repeat cases The balance shifts over the years from mostly first-time detections toward a much larger share of repeat detections.

Pandemic pivot During COVID-19 disruptions, first-time detections fell more sharply while repeat detections made up a bigger slice of the total.

Post-2022 recovery As services and enforcement return to normal, both first-time and repeat detections climb, with repeat growing faster and narrowing the gap.

Sources: Statistics Of Drug And Substance Abusers And Addicts By Case Status – Agensi Antidadah Kebangsaan (AADK)

What to expect in rehabilitation (Malaysia-specific)
Voluntary admission (PUSPEN / Cure & Care Clinics)
Intake, assessment, detox, counselling, medication-assisted treatment (for opiates), vocational training, and aftercare. Typical programme windows are ~6–24 months and tailored to individual needs. Voluntary clients at clinics are not subjected to legal action for presenting themselves.
